Trainable vs. Untrainable Models

What makes a model "learnable"? The presence of hyperparameters that can be tuned.

‚ùå Untrainable Models

No hyperparameters to tune

  • Simple Linear Regression (OLS)
  • Minimizes Œ£(y - ≈∑)¬≤ directly
  • One solution, no tuning needed

‚úÖ Trainable Models

  • Ridge/LASSO Regression (Œª penalty)

üéØ Key Insight

Hyperparameters control model complexity. Training = finding the best hyperparameter values using validation data. This is what makes ML models "self-learning".

How Machines Learn: The Training Loop

üîÑ The Learning Process

  1. Initialize model with random parameters
  2. Predict outcomes on training data
  3. Calculate prediction errors (loss)
  4. Adjust parameters to reduce error
  5. Repeat until error stops improving

Hyperparameters: Settings that control the learning process (learning rate, iterations, complexity)

Training & Validation Strategy

Why we split data: to estimate how well our model generalizes to unseen data

Simple Split:

Train (70%)
Test (30%)

With Validation (Two-Stage Process):

Step 1: Hold out Test set first

Train + Validation (80%)
Test (20%)

Step 2: Split the 80% via Cross-Validation

Train (~75% of 80%)
Val (~25%)

‚Üª K-Fold CV rotates which portion is validation

The Overfitting Problem

The central challenge in ML: finding the sweet spot between underfitting and overfitting

üìâ

Underfitting

Model too simple

High bias, misses patterns

"Can't even fit training data"

‚úÖ

Just Right

Optimal complexity

Good generalization

"Works on new data!"

üìà

Overfitting

Model too complex

High variance, memorizes noise

"Perfect on training, fails on test"

‚öñÔ∏è Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Bias: Error from wrong assumptions (underfitting) | Variance: Sensitivity to training data (overfitting)
